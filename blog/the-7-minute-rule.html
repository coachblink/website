The 7-Minute Rule: How to Start Something You're Dreading

There's a specific kind of stuck that has nothing to do with difficulty. You know exactly what to do. It's not hard. It might take fifteen minutes. And you still can't make yourself start it, not today, and somehow not yesterday either, even though yesterday you also knew it would only take fifteen minutes.

If you've felt that exact thing, the good news is it's not really about the task. It's about the size of the first move. "Clean the kitchen" is not a task, it's a category: it contains dishes, counters, the fridge, the trash, and an unknown number of decisions about what counts as clean enough. Your brain, reasonably, doesn't want to start a category. It wants to start a thing. Most of the time, nobody's handed you the thing, just the category, dressed up as a task.

Here's a rule that works better than most productivity advice, mostly because it's almost insultingly small. Pick something that takes seven minutes, and only commit to that. Not "clean the kitchen." Just: gather everything off the counter into one pile. That's it. Set an actual timer. When it goes off, you're done, and you're allowed to stop. No guilt, no "well now that I've started I should really finish."

Two things tend to happen. Sometimes you stop right at seven minutes, and that's a real, complete win: you did the thing you said you'd do, at the size you said you'd do it. Other times you keep going, because the hardest part of most tasks isn't the task itself, it's the activation energy of the first sixty seconds. Once you're standing in the kitchen with a pile of stuff in your hands, finishing the rest is a much smaller ask than starting from a cold stop on the couch was.

The number seven isn't magic. Five minutes works. Ten works for some people. The specific number matters less than two things: it has to be genuinely, almost comically small compared to what your brain thinks the task is, and it has to be timed, not open-ended. "For a while" doesn't give your brain a finish line to aim for, and a task with no finish line feels exactly like the category it came from.

What doesn't work as well is doing this planning in your head in the moment you're avoiding the task, because that's the same moment your brain is actively resisting the whole category. It's much easier to shrink a task down when you're not the one standing in front of it, avoiding it, right now. When you tell Blink's coach you're stuck on something, it doesn't hand you a pep talk. It hands you the seven-minute version and offers to start a timer right there, so the smallest possible version of the task is the only thing you have to decide to do.
