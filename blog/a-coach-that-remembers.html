A Coach That Remembers

Here's the thing that separates a coach from a chatbot, and it isn't how clever the replies are. It's whether it remembers you. A good human coach knows, three months in, that mornings are your only real focus window, that you always underestimate errands, that the word "quick" out of your mouth means it'll take an afternoon. You never have to re-explain yourself. Software that makes you re-explain yourself every session isn't coaching you, it's running customer support with a friendlier font.

So Blink's coach is built to remember. When you mention that phone calls drain you, or that you get your best work done before noon, that's not just noise in one conversation. It's something the coach can carry forward, so the next time it's helping you shape a day, it already knows to keep the calls out of your morning without being told again.

The harder half of memory is recall, and this is where most systems quietly fail. Remembering is easy. Remembering the right thing at the right moment is the whole game. Say you told Blink back in spring that you keep putting off cleaning the kitchen. Months later you ask, in a low moment, "why can I never make myself do the dishes?" You didn't say kitchen. You didn't say chores. A system that only matches the exact words you used comes back empty, as if you'd never said a thing. Blink is built to connect the two anyway, to understand that the dishes and the kitchen and "that stuff I always avoid" are the same thread, and to bring back what you already figured out last time instead of starting the conversation from zero.

That's the point of the memory, really. Not to have a longer transcript, but to stop making you solve the same problem twice. The advice that lands is almost never generic. It's the specific thing that worked for you in March, handed back to you in June when you'd forgotten you ever found it.

Which brings up the part we thought about hardest: a coach that remembers everything is also a coach that knows a lot about you, and that only works if the memory is yours. So it is. Memory is something you can turn off, and when it's off the coach genuinely doesn't reach back for any of it. When you delete something, it's gone from what the coach can recall, not just hidden from view. We'd rather build a coach that forgets on command than one that quietly hoards. Remembering the right thing is only worth anything if you're the one who decides what it gets to keep.
