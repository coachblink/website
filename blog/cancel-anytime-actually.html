Cancel Anytime, Actually

Most subscription apps say some version of "cancel anytime" somewhere in their marketing. Read that phrase closely and it usually means something narrower: you can cancel, technically, if you find the setting, which is sometimes three menus deep, sometimes behind an "are you sure?" screen designed to make you reconsider, sometimes only reachable by contacting support and waiting for a reply. None of that is illegal. It's also not "anytime" in any way a normal person would use the word.

This isn't a hypothetical complaint. It's one of the most common patterns across productivity and habit apps specifically: genuinely useful products that also make leaving deliberately annoying, on the theory that friction at the exit reduces churn. Sometimes it works, in the narrow sense that some people give up trying to cancel and keep paying by accident. That's not the same as building something people actually want to keep using.

We built Blink's subscription to be boring on purpose. Canceling is the same number of steps as subscribing, in the same place you'd expect to find it. No retention offer popping up to guilt you into staying, no maze of confirmation screens, no dark pattern doing quiet work in the background. If you don't want to keep paying for Blink, that should be a fact you can act on in under a minute, not a small project.

The honest reason for this isn't altruism. A subscription business built on people forgetting to cancel is optimizing for the wrong number. It looks like retention on a dashboard, and it's actually just friction, and friction-based retention has a way of showing up later as bad reviews, chargebacks, and people who quietly stop recommending you to anyone. A subscriber who stays because canceling is annoying was never really a subscriber. They were someone waiting for a free afternoon to deal with your app.

If Blink is useful to you, we'd rather you stay because it's useful, not because leaving costs more attention than it's worth. And if it's not useful to you, the fastest way to find out you were right to leave is to make leaving take the ten seconds it should.
