Streaks Punish the Wrong Day

Miss one day and the counter goes back to zero. That's the deal with streaks, and nearly every habit app ships them. Forty days of showing up, then one sick kid or one bad night of sleep, and the app now displays the same number it would show someone who never started.

The day after a miss is the most important day in the whole system. It's the day "I missed once" becomes either "I restarted" or "I used to use that app." And it's exactly the day a streak counter greets you with proof that your effort has been erased. The mechanic lands its hardest hit on you at your most likely-to-quit moment.

Streaks exist because the fear of losing one keeps people opening the app, and that looks great on an engagement dashboard. If you were going to be consistent anyway, the streak is a fun scoreboard. If your effort is real but uneven, it converts a single bad day into a verdict, and a lot of people respond to a verdict by leaving. If your consistency has always been the ragged kind, none of this is news to you.

Restarting is the actual skill. For most people, long-run consistency has never looked like an unbroken chain. It looks like showing up, missing some days, and coming back, so the coming-back is what deserves the confetti. In Blink, rewards only add: you earn points for what you did, and a missed day is a day you didn't earn points, not a fine collected against your history. Nothing you've built gets taken away because Tuesday went sideways.

We'll be honest about the trade. Punishing mechanics look better on the charts we're supposed to care about, at least for a while. We built the forgiving version anyway, for the same reason our cancellation flow is boring: an app made for people whose follow-through comes and goes shouldn't treat a gap as betrayal. We'd rather you come back in week six than feel guilty in week two.
