Why You Ignore Your Own Reminders

"Take out the trash" fires at 8pm. You're cooking, or you're mid-episode, or you're helping with homework, so you swipe it away and tell yourself you'll do it in a minute. At 8pm tomorrow it fires again. This time you swipe faster, because now you've had practice.

A reminder is a message from a past version of you who had good intentions and no information. Past-you picked 8pm weeks ago without knowing what any particular 8pm would actually contain. When the timing is wrong often enough, you stop reading the words on the notification and start clearing it on reflex, the same way you clear the badge on an app you don't remember installing. That's not a discipline problem. Your brain has correctly learned that most of these can be dismissed without anything bad happening, and it acts on what it learned.

The standard fix is volume: escalating alerts, repeating alarms, badges that climb into double digits. This works for about a week, then it makes everything worse, because you can't be forced to do a task at 8pm, but you can absolutely be trained to resent the thing doing the forcing. Gentle works better. Loud teaches you to ignore it.

Two changes matter more than any notification setting. The first is scarcity. A reminder should be rare enough that its arrival means something, so most tasks should live on your calendar as blocks you see when you look, and only the genuinely time-critical ones should get to interrupt you. The second is that the reminder should carry the first step, not the category. "Dentist" is a category. "Call Dr. Okafor's office, the number is in the March email" is something you can do while the phone is already in your hand.

And when you do swipe one away, what happens next is the whole game. An alarm clock just repeats itself. A system that's on your side notices the miss and renegotiates: tonight clearly didn't work, so when? "Same time tomorrow" is rarely the honest answer, and asking the question beats pretending the miss didn't happen.

That's how reminders work in Blink. They sit on the same calendar as everything else, so most tasks never need to ping you at all. When one does fire and you dismiss it, the coach treats that as information rather than failure: it offers a new slot and a smaller first step, which is usually what the swipe was really asking for. And if starting is the part you get stuck on, the seven-minute rule is the companion piece to this one.
